# Mike Lee

## Summary

Mike Lee is a U.S. Senator from Utah who opposes encryption backdoors, co-authored the Government Surveillance Reform Act with Ron Wyden and Zoe Lofgren, and has introduced bipartisan FISA reform legislation.

## Verified Facts

- U.S. Senator from Utah.[^1]
- Stated that federal encryption backdoor mandates are not worth the price in lost civil liberties.[^1]
- Argued that criminals could use foreign devices or independent encryption apps to evade mandated backdoors.[^1]
- Warned that weakening encryption would compromise online transactions and private information.[^1]
- Introduced the bipartisan Government Surveillance Reform Act with Wyden and Lofgren.[^2]
- The Government Surveillance Reform Act would reauthorize Section 702 with warrant requirements for U.S. person queries and limits on data-broker purchases.[^2]

[^2]: `SRC-LEE-GOVERNMENT-SURVEILLANCE-REFORM` — Lee Introduces Bipartisan Legislation to Secure Privacy Protections for Americans. Official Senate press release.

## Key Legislation

- **[Government Surveillance Reform Act](../../bibliography/people/SRC-LEE-GOVERNMENT-SURVEILLANCE-REFORM.md)** (co-author): Bipartisan FISA Section 702 reform with warrant protections and limits on purchasing Americans' data from brokers.
- **Opposition to encryption backdoors**: Public statements against federal mandates requiring device manufacturers to build government access into encrypted products.

## Historical Context

Lee has been a leading Republican voice on Fourth Amendment limits in the digital age, frequently partnering with Ron Wyden on surveillance reform. His encryption statements predate many contemporary platform and messaging debates but remain relevant to proposed backdoor mandates.

## Relevance to Open Source and Software Companies

Lee's surveillance reform bills would restrict warrantless government access to data held or generated by software platforms and purchased from commercial brokers. His opposition to encryption backdoors protects companies from being compelled to weaken security implementations in their products.
