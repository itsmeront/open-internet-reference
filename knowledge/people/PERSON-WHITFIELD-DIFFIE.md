# Whitfield Diffie

## Summary

Whitfield Diffie is a cryptographer and Turing Award laureate who co-invented public-key cryptography and has advocated for the legal right to use strong encryption.

## Verified Facts

- Co-inventor of public-key cryptography with Martin Hellman; co-author of the 1976 paper "New Directions in Cryptography."[^1]
- Consulting scholar at Stanford University's Center for International Security and Cooperation (CISAC).[^1]
- Former Sun Microsystems Fellow and Chief Security Officer; former Vice President for Information Security and Cryptography at ICANN.[^1]
- Co-author of *Privacy on the Line: The Politics of Wiretapping and Encryption* with Susan Landau.[^1]
- ACM Turing Award recipient (2015, with Martin Hellman).[^1]
- Marconi Fellow; recipient of the NIST/NSA National Computer Systems Security Award.[^1]


[^1]: `SRC-DIFFIE-CISAC` — Whitfield Diffie Stanford CISAC Profile. Self-reported profile; corroboration pending.

## Expert Testimony and Public Advocacy

Diffie spent much of the 1990s advocating for the individual and business right to use encryption, a debate that directly shaped the legal environment for software publishing and cryptographic exports. He remains a prominent public explainer of why key escrow, backdoors, and weakened cryptography create systemic risk.

## Relevance to Lawsuits Involving Software and Internet Infrastructure

Diffie is particularly useful when a case involves:

- Whether encryption features are technically feasible without backdoors
- Key management, authentication, or PKI design questions
- Historical and policy context for crypto export controls and software-as-speech debates
- Tradeoffs between surveillance mandates and system security

His work underpins modern TLS, code signing, and secure messaging — the same primitives at issue in many software and communications cases.
