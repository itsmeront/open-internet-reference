# Style Guide

## Editorial Voice

OIR should read like a careful public reference work: precise, neutral, sourced, and useful to both technical and legal readers.

## General Rules

- Prefer clear factual sentences over advocacy language.
- Separate what happened, what a source says, and what OIR concludes.
- Avoid implying certainty where the evidence is incomplete.
- Define technical and legal terms when first introduced.
- Use consistent names for organizations, cases, statutes, protocols, and technologies.
- Keep Axona-specific framing out of general reference pages unless it is directly relevant.

## Page Structure

Knowledge pages should generally include:

- Summary
- Metadata
- Verified facts (each bullet with a source footnote)
- Background or context (significant claims footnoted)
- Analysis, if appropriate
- Relationships
- Sources
- Research debt

## Citations in Prose

- Use Markdown footnotes for factual claims in Verified Facts and Historical Context.
- Do not present biography-only claims as fully verified; say “the official biography describes…” or add research debt for corroboration.
- Separate what a source says from what OIR has independently confirmed.

## Legal Writing

- Identify jurisdiction and authority level.
- Distinguish binding authority from persuasive authority.
- Avoid giving legal advice.
- Use direct citations for statutes, cases, regulations, and official records.

## Technical Writing

- Identify protocols, architectures, threat models, and assumptions.
- Distinguish implementation details from design principles.
- Avoid overstating security, privacy, or decentralization properties.

## Terminology

Use `internet` for the global network unless referring to a proper noun or quoted title. Use `open source` as an adjective unless quoting a source that uses another form.
