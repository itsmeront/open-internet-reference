# Tim Berners-Lee

## Summary

Sir Tim Berners-Lee invented the World Wide Web, founded the W3C, and holds faculty appointments at MIT and Oxford; he is a leading voice on open web standards, data sovereignty, and decentralized architectures such as Solid.

## Verified Facts

- Invented the World Wide Web at CERN (1989); wrote the first web client and server (1990).
- Defined foundational specifications for URIs, HTTP, and HTML.
- Founder and emeritus director of the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C).
- Emeritus 3Com Founders Professor of Engineering at MIT CSAIL; founded the Decentralized Information Group (DIG).
- Professor in the Computer Science Department at the University of Oxford; Honorary Student at Christ Church.
- Co-founder and CTO of Inrupt, promoting the open-source Solid platform for user-controlled data.
- Co-founder and president of the Open Data Institute (London).
- Recipient of the ACM Turing Award, Queen Elizabeth Prize for Engineering, and U.S. National Medal of Technology (with Vint Cerf and others).
- Author of *Weaving the Web* and *This is for Everyone* (2025).

## Expert Testimony and Public Advocacy

Berners-Lee has testified before U.S. congressional committees on the future of the Web, advocated for open standards and net-neutrality-style protections, and published widely on fixing the Web's business models and restoring user agency over personal data. His credibility combines direct invention of the Web's core protocols with decades of standards governance.

## Relevance to Lawsuits Involving Software and Internet Infrastructure

Berners-Lee is particularly useful when a case involves:

- HTTP, HTML, URI/URL standards and their intended semantics
- Web platform interoperability and open-standards obligations
- Data portability, personal data stores, and decentralized identity
- Historical context for how the Web layer relates to the underlying Internet (TCP/IP, DNS)
